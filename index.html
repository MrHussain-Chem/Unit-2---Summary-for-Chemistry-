Content Area A: Atomic Structure

Atomic number: Number of protons in the nucleus of an atom
Mass number: Total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus
Isotopes: Atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons
Relative atomic mass: Weighted average mass of all isotopes of an element relative to 1/12 the mass of carbon-12
Example: Calculating relative atomic mass
Chlorine has two isotopes: Cl-35 (75%) and Cl-37 (25%)
Ar = (35 √ó 75/100) + (37 √ó 25/100) = 26.25 + 9.25 = 35.5
üí° Application Tip:
Use atomic and mass numbers to find protons, neutrons, and electrons. For ions, adjust electrons based on charge.
Energy levels (shells): Fixed energy levels where electrons orbit the nucleus
Subshells: Subdivisions of energy levels (s, p, d, f)
Orbital: Region of space where there is a high probability of finding an electron
Electron filling rules:
  1. Aufbau principle: Fill lowest energy orbitals first
  2. Pauli exclusion principle: Maximum 2 electrons per orbital with opposite spins
  3. Hund's rule: Orbitals of equal energy filled singly before pairing
Example: Electronic configuration of Calcium (Ca, Z=20)
1s¬≤ 2s¬≤ 2p‚Å∂ 3s¬≤ 3p‚Å∂ 4s¬≤
üí° Application Tip:
Remember 4s fills before 3d! Order: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p...
First ionisation energy: Energy required to remove one mole of electrons from one mole of gaseous atoms to form one mole of gaseous 1+ ions
Equation format: X(g) ‚Üí X‚Å∫(g) + e‚Åª
Factors affecting ionisation energy:
  • Nuclear charge: Greater positive charge attracts electrons more strongly ‚Üí higher IE
  • Number of shells: More shells means outer electrons further from nucleus ‚Üí lower IE
  • Shielding: Inner electrons repel outer electrons, reducing effective nuclear charge ‚Üí lower IE
  • Subshells: Different subshells have different energies ‚Üí variations in IE trends
üí° Application Tip:
When explaining IE trends, mention ALL relevant factors: nuclear charge, distance from nucleus, and shielding.

Content Area B: Bonding & Structure

Metallic bonding: Electrostatic attraction between positive metal ions and delocalized electrons
Structure: Giant metallic lattice with layers of positive ions in a "sea" of delocalized electrons
Properties linked to structure:
  • High melting point: Strong metallic bonds throughout structure
  • Electrical conductivity: Mobile delocalized electrons carry charge
  • Malleability: Layers can slide over each other without breaking bonds
üí° Application Tip:
Always link properties to structure and bonding. For conductivity, mention "mobile delocalized electrons".
Ionic bonding: Electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions
Structure: Giant ionic lattice with ions in fixed positions
Properties:
  • High melting point: Strong ionic bonds throughout structure
  • Conducts when molten/dissolved: Ions become mobile and can carry charge
  • Brittle: Layers repel when displaced (like charges align)
üí° Application Tip:
Ionic compounds only conduct when ions are FREE TO MOVE (molten or dissolved), not when solid.
Covalent bonding: Electrostatic attraction between two nuclei and a shared pair of electrons between them
Dative covalent bond: Both electrons in shared pair come from one atom
Types of covalent bonds:
  • Single bond: One shared pair of electrons
  • Double bond: Two shared pairs of electrons
  • Triple bond: Three shared pairs of electrons
Molecular orbitals:
Sigma (œÉ) bond: Head-on overlap along axis between nuclei
Pi (œÄ) bond: Sideways overlap of p orbitals above and below axis
üí° Application Tip:
Bond strength relationship: Triple > Double > Single. Shorter bonds are stronger.
Core principle: Electron pairs repel each other and arrange to minimize repulsion

Electronegativity: Ability of an atom to attract the bonding pair of electrons in a covalent bond
Trend: Increases across period (increasing nuclear charge), decreases down group (increasing distance/shielding)
Determining polarity:
  • Bond polarity: Significant electronegativity difference ‚Üí polar bond (Œ¥+ and Œ¥-)
  • Molecular polarity: Polar bonds + asymmetric shape ‚Üí polar molecule
  • Even with polar bonds, symmetric molecules are non-polar (dipoles cancel)
Example: CO‚ÇÇ vs H‚ÇÇO polarity
CO‚ÇÇ: Linear, polar C=O bonds but dipoles cancel ‚Üí non-polar molecule
H‚ÇÇO: Bent shape, polar O-H bonds, dipoles don't cancel ‚Üí polar molecule
Three types (weakest to strongest):
  1. Van der Waals forces (London dispersion forces): Temporary dipole-induced dipole, present in ALL molecules
  2. Permanent dipole-permanent dipole: Between polar molecules, stronger than van der Waals forces
  3. Hydrogen bonding: Strongest IMF, only when H bonded to F/O/N
Hydrogen bonding (exact wording): Electrostatic attraction between a hydrogen atom bonded to F/O/N (Œ¥+) and a lone pair of electrons on F/O/N in a different molecule (Œ¥-)
üí° Application Tip:
Van der Waals forces and London dispersion forces are the same thing - both terms are correct.

Content Area C: Periodicity

Atomic radius trend: Decreases across period
  • Explanation: Nuclear charge increases, electrons in same shell, similar shielding, electrons pulled closer
Melting point trend: Na < Mg < Al (increases), Si (high), then P‚ÇÑ, S‚Çà, Cl‚ÇÇ, Ar (low)

Factors affecting rate (explain using collision theory):
  • Concentration: More particles per unit volume ‚Üí more frequent collisions ‚Üí faster rate
  • Pressure (gases): More particles in same space ‚Üí more frequent collisions ‚Üí faster rate
  • Temperature: Particles have more kinetic energy ‚Üí more frequent collisions AND more collisions exceed Ea ‚Üí faster rate
  • Surface area: More surface exposed ‚Üí more collisions possible ‚Üí faster rate
  • Catalyst: Provides alternative pathway with lower Ea ‚Üí more collisions exceed Ea ‚Üí faster rate
Catalyst: Speeds up rate of reaction without altering products, being itself unchanged chemically and in mass at end of reaction
Activation energy: Minimum energy required for a reaction to occur

Exothermic: ŒîH is negative, products have less energy than reactants, heat released

üí° Application Tip:
Check signs carefully! For formation route: products minus reactants. For combustion route: reactants minus products.
Dynamic equilibrium: In a closed system, the rate of forward reaction equals the rate of backward reaction, and concentrations of reactants and products remain constant (but both reactions continue)
Le Chatelier's Principle: When a system at equilibrium is subjected to a change, the position of equilibrium shifts to oppose that change
Effects on equilibrium:
  • Concentration increase: Equilibrium shifts to use up added substance
  • Pressure increase: Equilibrium shifts to side with fewer gas molecules
  • Temperature increase: Equilibrium shifts in endothermic direction
  • Catalyst: NO effect on position, but equilibrium reached faster

Content Area E: Organic Chemistry

Saturated hydrocarbon: Contains only single C-C bonds
Unsaturated hydrocarbon: Contains at least one C=C double bond
Homologous series: Family of compounds with same functional group, same general formula, similar chemical properties, gradual change in physical properties
General formulae to memorize:
  • Alkanes: C‚ÇôH‚ÇÇ‚Çô‚Çä‚ÇÇ

  • Alcohols: C‚ÇôH‚ÇÇ‚Çô‚Çä‚ÇÅOH
IUPAC naming rules (up to 6 carbons):
  1. Identify longest carbon chain (parent name)
  2. Number from end giving lowest numbers
  3. Name substituents with position numbers
  4. Multiple identical: di-, tri-, tetra-
  5. List alphabetically
üí° Application Tip:
Parent names: methane, ethane, propane, butane, pentane, hexane. Check spelling!
Structural isomerism: Same molecular formula, different structural arrangement
  • Chain isomers: Different carbon skeletons
  • Position isomers: Functional group in different positions
  • Functional group isomers: Different functional groups
E/Z isomerism (stereoisomerism):
  • Requires C=C double bond (no rotation)
  • Two different groups on each carbon of double bond
  • E isomer: Higher priority groups on opposite sides
  • Z isomer: Higher priority groups on same side
  • Priority by atomic number (Cahn-Ingold-Prelog rules)
